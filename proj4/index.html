Overview

During this project, we developed a point mass mesh and shading system to model the behavior and visualization of cloth materials with different parameters, including pinned points, object collisions, and shaders. In part 1, we coded the creation of the point mass grid itself and we modeled the internal dynamics of the cloth using structural, bending, and shearing springs. We had some confusion about horizontal versus vertical cloths and how they worked with xy indices given the different coordinate system terminology, but forum questions and the project spec helped resolve these issues. We also avoided mesh edge issues by hard-coding index checks to ensure springs were only created where they would not erroneously reach beyond the mesh. In part 2, we coded the simulation of forces and stretching constraints to allow the cloth’s position to evolve from one timestep to another through the movement of its point masses. In part 3, we coded collisions between the cloth and planes or spheres, such that point masses in the cloth mesh could approach the objects’ borders but never physically pass through. We initially encountered bugs with detecting whether points were crossing from one side of a plane to another as well as with keeping points on the correct side while properly incorporating friction and the given surface offset. By using forum posts and debugging the correction vectors being applied to the point masses, we were able to track down the issues with the plane collision function and patch them. In part 4, we coded the self-collision of the cloth by having point masses in the cloth mesh repulse each other if they came too close together based on given parameters. We found issues with the cloth passing through planes and not repulsing its points enough, but we were able to trace the former back to issues with the plane collision function, and the latter was resolved with some refactoring of the code. Finally, in part 5, we coded a variety of shader programs to better render the cloth. These programs, written in GLSL, define the shape and color of the provided geometry. This part ended up being relatively simple to implement, since it was mostly filling in the provided formulas. We did encounter a few bugs, mainly related to needing to normalize vectors and taking care to convert 4D vectors to 3D. By the end of the project, we had created a system that simulated the physics of cloth materials, including gravity, pinned points, spring forces, internal constraints, object collision, and self-collision; allowed for the modeling of different materials through the variation of parameters like damping, density, spring constants, and shaders; and visualized the evolution of simulated systems across timesteps and with different textures.

Part 1

For part 1, we built a grid of masses and springs to represent a cloth mesh. We began by building the grid through nested loop iteration over the ‘xy’ coordinate indices (which was x and y for vertical meshes but x and z for horizontal meshes). We offset the indices by 0.5 from the beginning and end to center the cloth points, such that for a cloth with width 5 and 10 num_width_points, the points would range {0.25, 0.75, 1.25,…,4.75} in the x direction rather than starting at 0.0 or ending at 5.0. For horizontal meshes we set all every point’s y values to 1, and for vertical meshes we randomized every point’s z value to be between 0.001 and -0.001. We also checked to see if each point’s xy indices were contained in the ‘pinned’ vector, and if so, we marked those points as pinned. We placed every new point at the back of the point_masses vector, and since the inner nested loop was in the x-direction, this created a row-major (x-axis) organization in the vector. We then looped through every point in the vector and, based on the xy indices of the point, created structural, shearing, and bending springs to connect it to other points in the mesh, with index checking to ensure springs stopped at the borders of the mesh. Part 2

For part 2, we simulated forces on each point in the cloth, used timestep Verlet integration to compute their next positions based on these forces, and constrained the points not to exceed the rest length of the springs connecting them. We began by iterating over all point masses and summing the forces over each point: first the external forces, such as external acceleration from gravity, and then adding all spring forces from the structural, bending, and shearing constraints applied between the given point and other points, where spring forces were applied half-half between each point. We then looped through all points, calculated acceleration from net forces and mass, and determined the next position of the point based on its current position, its existing velocity (from its last position and its current position), and its directional acceleration. After a new position was calculated, our final update was to constrain each point’s position to ensure it never deviated more than 10% beyond the rest length of the springs around it.

We changed several parameters during simulation to understand how the simulated properties of the cloth affected its behavior. When we applied a very low spring constant (ks), cloth appeared to bounce constantly, local sections of fabric forming hills and valleys with each timestep before being pulled back into the planar formation of the mesh. At higher spring constant values, cloths were very stable and planar, with all points in the mesh moving in the same general direction and preserving their orientation and shape. This was likely due to how higher spring constants allowed points to much more forcefully pull each other back into planarity, preventing local maxima and minima and general bouncy behavior, while lower spring constants allowed significant local deformations, as seen in the image below.

Higher densities led to a more pronounced gravitational effect, with the cloth settling down to its rest position far quicker with higher density; however, lower density allowed the cloth to stay suspended for longer, as seen in the image below.

With lower damping, the forces from the spring constants increased, leading to significant deformations; this is seen in the image below. Meanwhile, higher damping led to quick settling of the cloth due to a lack of rest position bouncing.

The two images below show pinned4.json, with default parameters, in its final resting state. The 4 corners are pinned, allowing the edges to be slightly curved in suspension, while the center of the cloth is depressed relative to the rest of it (global minima).

Part 3

For part 3, we implemented physics for handling collisions between point masses in cloth meshes and objects (specifically, planes and spheres). We implemented collision-checking after force summation and Verlet integration by checking whether each point had collided with objects in the scene. For spheres, we checked if points’ new positions were within the radius of the sphere, and if so, we computed a correction vector that set the points’ new positions slightly beyond the radius (further away with higher friction). For planes, we checked if a point’s new position would change which side of the plane it was on, and if so, we computed a correction vector that similarly set the points’ new positions slightly beyond the plane at the tangent point where it would have intersected the plane following its previous path, based on a surface offset variable and based on friction (further away with higher friction). The at-rest states of the cloth falling on a sphere, at different spring constants (ks = 50,000; ks = 5,000; and ks = 500; shown respectively below). Higher spring constants seemed to yield quicker equilibration of forces, fewer folds, and more suspended positions in the final state, as indicated by the relatively simple configuration of the cloth with ks = 50,000 compared to the complex, heavily-folded, downward-facing cloth with ks = 500. At higher spring constants, the cloth reached this final state in far fewer timesteps than at lower spring constants, likely because the more weak spring forces in the latter configuration allowed minor disturbances (such as the interaction of gravity, collisions, and rest length corrections) to more easily overcome the forces in the spring mesh, so only when most forces had been settled and most velocities were near zero did the spring mesh forces themselves actually take over and establish an equilibrium.

The image below simply shows a shaded cloth resting peacefully on a flat plain. Coding plane collisions proved more difficult than expected - our initial formula for offsetting the final position of points from the plane turned out to be wrong, as it used the surface offset variable and friction incorrectly. We only discovered this when we attempted to code self-collisions, because the vertical cloth fell almost straight through the plane, at which point we returned to the collision code to fix it.

Part 4

In part 4, we coded for self-collisions between point masses within the same cloth mesh based on a given particle thickness parameter. At each timestep, we broke up the coordinate system into bounding boxes, each of which was identified by a unique float hash. We then used a hashmap that mapped those bounding boxes’ floats to vectors of point masses, and added each point to its respective hashmapped bounding box vector. We were then able to efficiently check for self-collisions between points in the same bounding box, as determined by if the distance between two points was too small; for every given point we added a correction vector for each other point it was too close to, and we averaged the correction vectors over all points and simulation steps to create self-collision repulsion.

The result can be seen below in 3 phases based on our simulation of selfCollision.json with default parameters. In the first phase, the cloth falls vertically onto the plane, rippling as the impact propagates and folding inwards onto itself as the plane’s normal force clashes with the self-collision repulsion we coded.

In the second phase, seen below, the cloth finishes falling and begins to fold and unfold on top of itself as gravity, the plane’s normal force, self-collision repulsion, spring forces, and internal constraints all battle to push every point mass in the cloth one way or another. During this phase, the cloth slowly transitions from bouncy instability to planar equilibration, and its folds appear and disappear sporadically as it mechanically works itself out.

In the final phase, seen below, the cloth finally finishes unfolding itself as much as is possible, given the starting conditions, and settles into a simple configuration that is permanently stable.

When we varied the density and spring constant of the cloth and ran the same simulation, the cloth exhibited different behavior. When density was set to be two orders of magnitude higher, we found that the gravitational force overpowered all other forces. During the first phase, this allowed gravity to force the cloth down to the plane much more quickly than the spring forces could propagate the impact through the cloth, so initial ripples only appeared close to the ground, as seen to the left below. The gravity was also enough that in the third phase, the final resting state of the cloth was far more folded than with default parameters, as shown to the right below; the spring forces and self-collisions were not strong enough to overcome gravity, although the cloth continued to ripple locally due to these forces even after supposedly reaching a rest position.

When we varied the spring constant, we found that lower spring constants led to far more spreading and messier folding (both initially and at-rest), as the cloth was less able to pull itself together into a cohesive mass based on the spring constraints. Similarly to with large density, during the first phase, the cloth’s initial fall did not significantly propagate impact ripples, since the spring forces were too weak to pull point masses from higher up, as seen on the left below. During the second phase, we observed far more ripples, folds, and spreading in the cloth, and it took a significantly longer amount of time for the cloth to unfold itself (although it did eventually do so) because the contribution of the spring forces were too weak to overcome gravity quickly, as seen on the right below. When the cloth finally equilibrated, the low spring constant did not affect what the final resting state looked like, except for more local ripples on the folded side of the cloth, as seen at the bottom image.

Part 5

In part 5 we implemented a variety of shader programs. GLSL shaders determine how the given geometries are rendered to the screen, controlling various aspects of the geometry and its colors. Two basic shaders are vertex shaders and fragment shaders. Vertex shaders operate on the geometry itself, modifying the position and normal of the geometry’s vertices. These operations are done before rasterization. Bump and displacement mapping are examples of vertex shaders. After rasterization, the fragment shaders control how the geometry is colored. Using the geometry calculated by the vertex shaders, these shaders output the final color. Blinn-Phong shading and texture mapping are examples of fragment shaders.

Blinn-Phong Shading

Blinn-Phong shading is one of the more advanced options for fragment shading. This model combines ambient, diffuse, and specular lighting components to output a final image. The ambient component simulates ambient lighting on the object, which is a constant amount of illumination across the entire object. The diffuse component uses the standard diffuse lighting equation to calculate the lighting on the various surfaces of the object, lighting which varies based on the orientation of the surface with respect to the light. Surfaces whose normals are in line with the direction of the light are brighter. The specular component simulates the bright spot that can occur on shiny objects on surfaces reflecting light directly towards the camera. These three components can be seen individually below, as well as the final product.

Texture Mapping

Texture mapping is very simple. The uv coordinates of a vertice is used to sample a provided texture map, returning the final color.

Bump and Displacement Mapping

Bump and displacement mapping are two options for adding additional geometry to a surface. Bump mapping alters the normal of a surface to equal the normal of the provided texture geometry, which is calculated by observing small changes in the texture. This gives the surface the illusion of detail, without changing the actual geometry. Displacement mapping actually changes the location of the vertices. The same technique of changing the normals in bump mapping is used, with the addition of a vertex shader which changes the position to match the provided texture geometry. This results in a more realistic result, as the actual surface changes in shape. This is particularly apparent when looking at the edges of the cloth or the surface of the sphere, as the additional texture breaks up what would be a smooth surface.

Below, we see bump mapping and displacement mapping for two different coarseness values. Bump mapping shows little difference between the two values. Displacement mapping, however, shows a large difference, with the sphere appearing noticeably jagged at a low coarseness value. At a high value, the surface appears much smoother, and the displacement matches the given texture.
